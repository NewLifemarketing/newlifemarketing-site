Paid Media Management: Who Should Run It and What to Pay

Paid Media/Google Ads/Meta Ads

by Cruz Lee Posted on July 23, 2026

Every agency pitch deck says the same thing: "we manage your paid media." That phrase tells you nothing about the actual decision in front of you — whether to hire in-house, bring on a freelancer, or sign with an agency, and what each option should realistically cost.

Paid media management is the ongoing work of testing what's converting and reallocating budget toward it — not a one-time campaign setup. The harder question isn't what that work involves. It's who's actually positioned to do it well for your business, and what's a fair price for that.

Setup Isn't Management, and That Gap Is Where Money Gets Wasted

Plenty of businesses hire someone to launch a Google Ads or Meta Ads campaign, watch the dashboard for a week, and call it done. That's setup. Management is what happens after — and the gap between the two is exactly where wasted spend piles up: keywords that stopped converting weeks ago, an audience that's gone stale, budget still flowing to both because nobody caught it in time.

Think about a home renovation company spending $2,000 a month on Google Ads. In week one, three keywords eat 80% of the budget and produce zero calls. Managed properly, that gets caught in the weekly review and rerouted to the search terms that are actually converting. Left alone, the same $2,000 burns the same way every month.

In-House, Freelancer, or Agency: the Real Decision

There's no universally right answer, but there's a useful filter: how much time can someone realistically spend on this every single week, and do they have the platform-specific judgment to act on what the data is telling them?

  • In-house works when someone on your team already understands ad platforms and can genuinely dedicate hours weekly — not "whenever there's time." Ad platforms change often enough that a part-time, distracted approach falls behind fast.
  • Freelancers can be a reasonable middle ground for a single platform and a tight budget, but coordination across multiple channels (Google, Meta, sometimes both at once) often falls through the cracks with a single generalist.
  • Agencies earn their fee when they're running tested processes across many accounts, not just because they have a nicer dashboard.

Whichever option you're evaluating, ask them to walk through a typical week on your account: who decides how budget moves between campaigns, who cuts underperforming creative and how fast, who adjusts targeting based on real conversion data instead of a demographic guess, and who turns the monthly report into an actual decision instead of a PDF nobody reads. If the honest answer to any of those is "nobody, really," that option is monitoring your account, not managing it.

Say you run a small e-commerce store selling outdoor gear. Whoever ends up managing your account should be testing product images against each other every week and cutting the losers fast — not glancing at the dashboard once a month and calling it good.

What Each Option Actually Costs

  • In-house costs a salary or part of one, plus a learning curve — the real cost is often the weeks it takes someone new to get good enough at a platform to stop wasting money while learning it.
  • Freelancers typically charge a flat monthly rate or hourly fee that scales with how many platforms and accounts they're covering — cheaper than an agency at small budgets, but a single person has a ceiling on how many accounts they can genuinely stay on top of.
  • Agencies are typically priced as a flat monthly fee, a percentage of ad spend, or some blend of the two — and the exact number varies widely by agency and account size. Whatever the pricing model, the real question isn't the number — it's what you get for it: a documented testing cadence, a report that names specific changes made and why, and a clear owner who can explain what's working and what got cut.

FAQ

Should I hire in-house or use an agency for paid media management?

It depends on whether someone on your team can realistically dedicate real weekly hours to it and already understands the platforms. If not, a freelancer or agency with an established weekly process will outperform a distracted part-time effort almost every time.

How much should paid media management cost?

Pricing varies by agency and ad spend level — some charge a flat monthly fee, others a percentage of spend, and there's no single industry-wide number to point to. What matters more than the price itself is whether the fee includes active weekly testing and reallocation, not just campaign monitoring.

How do I know if my current paid media management is actually working?

Ask whoever runs your account to name the specific changes made in the last month — budget moved, creative cut, targeting adjusted. If nobody can name one, you're paying for monitoring, not management.

Where This Leaves You

Paid media management isn't a feature list — it's a weekly discipline of testing, cutting, and reallocating. The decision that actually matters isn't what the work involves, it's who's positioned to do it well for your business and what's fair to pay them for it.

  • Launching a campaign is setup. Testing and reallocating budget every week is management — and the gap between the two is where wasted spend hides.
  • Choose in-house, freelancer, or agency based on who can realistically dedicate real weekly time and platform-specific judgment to your account.
  • Ask any option to name specific changes made to your account in the last month — if they can't, you're paying for monitoring, not management.
  • Pricing varies widely by model and account size — judge the fee by what it buys, not the number itself.
